Masaya is a shield volcano situated within the Masaya Caldera, approximately 20 km southeast of Managua. It rises to an elevation of 594 m and is one of the most persistently active volcanoes in Central America. The volcano complex comprises multiple craters, of which Santiago is the most active. Since the early 1990s, Santiago has hosted a near-permanent lava lake that emits exceptionally high volumes of sulfur dioxide (SO₂), making Masaya one of the largest continuous volcanic SO₂ sources on Earth. The gas plume frequently affects surrounding communities and agricultural areas, causing acid rain and crop damage. Masaya is also a national park and a major tourist destination, allowing visitors to observe the lava lake from the crater rim.

Activity Timeline

  • 1994 – Lava lake re-establishes in Santiago crater after a period of reduced activity; strong SO₂ degassing resumes
  • 1997 – Elevated SO₂ flux recorded; minor ash ejections from Santiago crater
  • 2001 – Intense lava lake activity; incandescent spattering and gas-driven explosions observed
  • 2003 – Temporary drainage of lava lake followed by rapid resurgence; increased seismicity
  • 2008 – Renewed lava lake visibility after partial obstruction by rubble; heightened degassing
  • 2012 – Minor phreatic explosions; ballistic ejecta reported near crater rim
  • 2015 – Significant intensification of lava lake; SO₂ emissions among the highest recorded
  • 2016 – Major resurgence; lava lake expands and brightens, drawing international scientific attention
  • 2018 – Ash emissions and small explosions; national park temporarily closed
  • 2020 – Persistent lava lake activity continues; gas monitoring network expanded
  • 2023 – Ongoing degassing and lava lake fluctuations; seismic swarms recorded
