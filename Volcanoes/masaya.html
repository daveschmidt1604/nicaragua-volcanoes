Masaya is a shield volcano situated within the Masaya Caldera, approximately 20 km southeast of Managua. It rises to an elevation of 594 m and is one of the most persistently active volcanoes in Central America. The volcano complex comprises multiple craters, of which Santiago is the most active. Since the early 1990s, Santiago has hosted a recurrently active lava lake — most recently re-established in August 2016 — that emits exceptionally high volumes of sulfur dioxide (SO₂), making Masaya one of the largest continuous volcanic SO₂ sources on Earth. The gas plume frequently affects surrounding communities and agricultural areas, causing acid rain and crop damage. Masaya is also a national park and a major tourist destination, allowing visitors to observe the lava lake from the crater rim.

Recent Activity

Masaya has been continuously active since at least the 16th century, when Spanish conquistadors observed its lava lake. The following records highlight notable eruptions and activity episodes from recent decades. For the complete eruption history, see the GVP entry ↗.

  • 1993–1994 – Lava lake in Santiago crater gradually cools; persistent SO₂ degassing continues at reduced intensity
  • 1997 – November 17: small explosion in Santiago crater ejects lava bombs up to 50 cm onto the western crater rim; SO₂ flux measured at up to 363 metric tons/day
  • 1998 – September 14: small explosion in Santiago crater again ejects lava bombs up to 50 cm onto the western rim
  • 1999–2000 – November 22, 1999: thermal anomaly detected by satellite; several small ash eruptions from Santiago crater continue through January 2000
  • 2001 – April 23: major explosion forms new vent in Santiago crater floor; blocks up to 60 cm ejected up to 500 m from the crater rim; one of the largest eruptions in decades
  • 2003 – October 4: eruption cloud rises to ~4.6 km altitude; intermittent ash eruptions continue through early 2005
  • 2006 – August: two small phreatomagmatic eruptions; October 21–23: new vent opens on Santiago crater floor containing a small lava lake with intense degassing
  • 2008 – Ash eruptions in April (plume to 2.1 km), June, August, October (4.6 km), and December (5.3–6.1 km)
  • 2012 – April 30 – May 17: ~68 explosions from Santiago crater; initial explosion ejects blocks with ash column reaching ~1 km above the summit; ashfall in surrounding park areas
  • 2015 – October: current eruption period begins; elevated activity and degassing at Santiago crater
  • 2016 – August: lava lake returns to Santiago crater with large thermal anomalies; heightened degassing draws international scientific attention
  • 2018 – Ash emissions and small explosions; Masaya National Park temporarily closed to visitors
  • 2020 – Persistent lava lake activity; continuous SO₂ flux measurements maintained
  • 2023 – Ongoing lava lake fluctuations and seismic swarms; eruption considered ongoing by GVP
