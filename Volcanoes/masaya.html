Masaya is a shield volcano situated within the Masaya Caldera, approximately 20 km southeast of Managua. It rises to an elevation of 594 m and is one of the most persistently active volcanoes in Central America. The volcano complex comprises multiple craters, of which Santiago is the most active. Since the early 1990s, Santiago has hosted a recurrently active lava lake — most recently re-established in December 2015 — that emits exceptionally high volumes of sulfur dioxide (SO₂), making Masaya one of the largest continuous volcanic SO₂ sources on Earth. The gas plume frequently affects surrounding communities and agricultural areas, causing acid rain and crop damage. Masaya is also a national park and a major tourist destination, allowing visitors to observe the lava lake from the crater rim.

Recent Activity

Masaya has been continuously active since at least the 16th century, when Spanish conquistadors observed its lava lake. The following records highlight notable eruptions and activity episodes from recent decades. For the complete eruption history, see the GVP entry ↗.

  • 1993 June – 1994 November: small ash eruption on 16 June leads to lava lake formation at ~30–40 m depth; lake becomes crusted with incandescent window active by August; lava lake gradually cools through November 1994 with persistent SO₂ degassing
  • 1996 December: Strombolian eruption on 5 December; bombs up to 10 cm, ash, and Pele's hair ejected from Santiago crater; partial collapse of inner crater walls; vent gas temperature drops from 1,084°C to 360°C
  • 1997 June – 1997 November: renewed eruptive activity beginning June 3; elevated SO₂ degassing; small explosion on 17 November ejects lava bombs up to 50 cm onto the western crater rim; SO₂ flux up to 363 t/day
  • 1998 September: small explosion on 14 September ejects lava bombs up to 50 cm onto the western crater rim
  • 1999 November – 2000 March: thermal anomaly detected by satellite in November 1999; several small ash eruptions from Santiago crater through March 2000
  • 2001 April: major explosion on 23 April; new 10 m diameter vent opens on the Santiago crater floor; blocks up to 60 cm ejected up to 500 m from the crater rim; one of the largest eruptions at Masaya in decades
  • 2003 September – 2003 December: eruptive activity beginning September; eruption cloud rises to ~4.6 km altitude on 4 October; intermittent ash eruptions through December
  • 2005 March: renewed ash eruptions; crater floor collapses recorded; activity ends March 2005
  • 2006 August – 2006 October: seismic tremor increases from 3 August; two small phreatomagmatic eruptions on 4 August producing dark grey ash; small ash ejections also recorded on 6 August; new vent forms on Santiago crater floor during 21–23 October with small lava lake and intense degassing
  • 2008 April – December: intermittent ash eruptions throughout the year; notable plume heights of 2.1 km on 29 April, 4.6 km on 9 October, and 5.3–6.1 km on 17 December
  • 2012 April – June: ~68 explosions from Santiago crater between 30 April and 17 May; initial explosion ejects blocks up to 60 cm with ash column to ~1 km above summit; ash deposits up to 2 mm thickness in park areas; fires on crater flanks; Masaya Volcano National Park temporarily closed; activity continues through June
  • 2015 October: renewed eruptive activity begins 3 October; lava lake rises to the surface on 11 December, becoming visible and audible from the crater rim; vent widens and SO₂ emissions increase significantly; onset of the current eruptive episode, ongoing to the present
  • 2016: peak lava lake activity within the current eruptive period; maximum SO₂ flux up to 50.7 kg/s recorded December 2015 – February 2016
  • 2019 March – July: landslide in southern sector of Santiago crater on 2 March completely covers the lava lake and obstructs the main vent; Masaya Volcano National Park temporarily closed as safety precaution; activity declines through mid-year; small explosion on 21 July produces gas-and-ash plume drifting NW and W
  • 2020 – 2021: declining lava lake level; crater wall collapses and landslides toward the lava lake; increased degassing episodes recorded by INETER
  • 2022 – present: lava lake temperature declining (~300°C in August 2022, ~294°C by September 2022); SO₂ flux 1,334 t/day (October 2022); eruption ongoing per GVP with continued INETER monitoring
