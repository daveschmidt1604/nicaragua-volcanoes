Overview

Cerro Negro is the youngest volcano in Central America, having first erupted in 1850. It is a basaltic cinder cone rising to 728 m elevation, located approximately 20 km northeast of León. Unlike the older stratovolcanoes in the Maribios chain, Cerro Negro is built almost entirely of loose scoria and basaltic lava, giving it its characteristic black appearance and steep flanks. Its eruptions are typically Strombolian to sub-Plinian in style, producing lava flows, ash columns, and large volumes of tephra. Since its formation it has erupted more than 25 times, making it one of the most active volcanoes in Nicaragua. The volcano has also become well known for the activity of "volcano boarding" — sliding down its steep black scoria slopes.

Recent Activity

Cerro Negro first erupted in 1850 and has since produced more than 25 eruptions, making it one of the most active volcanoes in Central America. The following records highlight notable eruptions and activity episodes from recent decades. For the complete eruption history, see the GVP entry ↗.

  • 1992 April: VEI 3 violent Strombolian to sub-Plinian eruption; main phase 9–12 April, resumed at lower intensity 13–14 April; ash clouds to ~7–7.5 km altitude; ~4 cm of tephra in León (18 km WSW); ~28,000 people evacuated 10–12 April; 15 houses and several other structures collapse in León and surrounding areas due to tephra load; tephra volume ~2.6 × 10⁷ m³
  • 1995 May – 1995 December: VEI 2 two-phase eruption; Phase 1 (29 May – mid-August, ~79 days): Strombolian explosions with ash columns 200–1,000 m; small pyroclastic flow on NW flank 2 June; lava flow extends ~1.5 km north; ~6,000 evacuated; 95-day hiatus; Phase 2 (mid-November – 3 December, 13 days): larger explosions ejecting bombs up to 2 m diameter
  • 1999 August – 1999 December: VEI 1 eruption triggered by Mw 5.2 earthquake swarm; four new vents open near Cristo Rey on the S flank, building cones ~40 m high; lava fountains reach ~300 m; ash clouds to ~7,000 m altitude; activity ends 10 December
  • 2011: elevated seismic tremor recorded throughout the year; ~240 VT earthquakes documented by INETER; passive degassing from summit fumaroles and 1999 cinder cones; no confirmed eruption
  • 2013 June: seismic swarm on 4 June; 49 earthquakes detected (too small to locate); RSAM increases ~60 units; INETER maintains Alert Status Green; no confirmed eruption
  • 2015: elevated seismic unrest recorded by INETER; no confirmed eruption
  • 2019: elevated seismic unrest and increased fumarolic degassing; INETER raises alert level
  • 2023: ongoing background seismic unrest; continuous INETER monitoring
