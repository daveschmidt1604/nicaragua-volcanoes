Cerro Negro is the youngest volcano in Central America, having first erupted in 1850. It is a basaltic cinder cone rising to 728 m elevation, located approximately 20 km northeast of León. Unlike the older stratovolcanoes in the Maribios chain, Cerro Negro is built almost entirely of loose scoria and basaltic lava, giving it its characteristic black appearance and steep flanks. Its eruptions are typically Strombolian to sub-Plinian in style, producing lava flows, ash columns, and large volumes of tephra. Since its formation it has erupted more than 25 times, making it one of the most active volcanoes in Nicaragua. The volcano has also become well known for the activity of "volcano boarding" — sliding down its steep black scoria slopes.

Activity Timeline

  • 1992 – Major sub-Plinian eruption immediately preceding the study period; lava flows and heavy tephra fall
  • 1995 – Eruption with lava flows on the western flank; ash column reaches several kilometers height
  • 1999 – Renewed eruptive activity; lava flows and Strombolian explosions recorded
  • 2007 – Increased fumarolic activity and minor seismic swarms; alert level raised
  • 2011 – Eruption with minor lava emission and ash plumes; INETER monitors closely
  • 2015 – Elevated seismic unrest and fumarolic intensification; no major eruption but heightened alert
  • 2019 – Increased degassing and minor explosive activity; tourist access temporarily restricted
  • 2023 – Ongoing seismic monitoring; volcano remains in a state of background unrest
